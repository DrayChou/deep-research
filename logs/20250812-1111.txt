                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              Shared neoantigens, such such as those derived from high-frequency driver mutations like PI3K and IDH1R132H, are highly specific to tumors and possess strong immunogenicity, making them ideal targets for universal cancer vaccines. They are not expressed in normal tissues, thus avoiding self-immunity [ - ], []. However, their efficacy can be limited by tumor heterogeneity, poor immunogenicity, and immune tolerance [Therapeutic cancer vaccines: advancements, challenges and ...].

Tumor-associated antigens (TAAs), like prostate acid phosphatase targeted by Sipuleucel-T for metastatic resistant prostate cancer, are highly expressed in cancer cells but also present in normal tissues [Therapeutic cancer vaccines: advancements, challenges and ...]. This shared expression can lead to central and peripheral immune tolerance, resulting in lower vaccine efficiency and potential autoimmunity []. WT1 and Her2/neu are other examples of TAAs [].

Oncoviral antigens, such as those from Human Papillomavirus (HPV) or Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV), are highly specific and immunogenic because they are foreign proteins expressed in tumor cells due to viral integration [- PMC], [Eliciting antitumor immunity via therapeutic cancer vaccines - Nature]. Preventive vaccines, like those for HPV-mediated cervical cancer, have proven effective [].

A novel universal strategy, developed by Professor Yongfeng Jin's team at Zhejiang University in January 2025, involves an mRNA vaccine that reprograms tumor cells to express known pathogen antigens (e.g., ovalbumin (OVA), Hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg), SARS-CoV-2 spike protein) [Advanced Science ...], [A Universal Strategy of Anti-Tumor mRNA Vaccine by Harnessing ...]. This approach leverages existing "off-the-shelf" immunity to these common antigens, activating pre-existing antigen-specific T cells to kill tumor cells. This strategy demonstrated significant tumor growth inhibition and reshaped the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment in mouse models [A Universal Strategy of Anti-Tumor mRNA Vaccine by Harnessing ...].
